## MoP-JEPA: Hard-Assigned Predictor Mixtures for Stochastic JEPA World Models 

Zhi Song 1,2 , Ximing Xing 2, Zhenchao Tang 2, Hanbo Huang 2, Weilong Yan 2, Tianxu Lv 2, Minghao Yang 2, Zhongzheng Niu 2, Bing He *2 , Lusheng Wang *1 , Jianhua Yao *2

Abstract 

JEPA world models commonly predict the next latent state with one regressor. Under stochastic transitions, squared and cosine regression return the conditional mean and its normal-ized direction, respectively: a single compromise that may match no valid successor. MoP-JEPA instead uses K hard-assigned heads and a context-only router to produce a finite candidate set in one pass. On held-out OGBench transitions, graph search with single-output predictors succeeds on 0.02 –

0.09 of queries, whereas MoP-JEPA reaches 0.85 . To dis-tinguish useful successors from indiscriminate coverage, we also measure verified-route success ( realroute ), which checks after graph construction whether the proposal contains a path of real transitions. MoP-JEPA leads this same-protocol met-ric on all three mazes; an MDN attains high raw coverage but predicts many nonexistent edges. 

Introduction 

JEPA world models plan by rolling a latent predictor forward (LeCun 2022; Zhou et al. 2024; Assran et al. 2025). They work well when the future is nearly deterministic. Stochas-tic dynamics violate this assumption. One context can have several valid successors, and planning requires those succes-sors rather than their average. A deterministic JEPA predictor trained by squared re-gression returns the conditional mean; with normalized tar-gets and cosine loss, it returns the mean direction. A gated weighted-sum MoE still emits one vector per context and therefore retains the same single-output restriction. MoP-JEPA replaces that output with a predictor containing K

hard-assigned heads. Each target updates its nearest head, while a router learns which heads are active from context alone. The resulting candidate set can be used by graph search or MPC. Figure 1 summarizes the argument. We first characterize the single-output restriction of de-terministic and fused-MoE predictors and relate hard as-signment to conditional vector quantization (Props. 1–3). Trained models are then examined in latent and decoded space, with additional tests on antmaze, image observa-tions, ETH/UCY, SVHN, and DINO-WM. Finally, we de-termine whether the candidate successors form usable tran-sitions for planning. Because a large candidate set can inflate coverage without learning context dependence, the evalu-ation includes a context-free codebook, shuffled contexts,

Related Work 

JEPA world models. I-JEPA (Assran et al. 2023) and V-JEPA (Bardes et al. 2024) established latent-regression pre-diction. DINO-WM (Zhou et al. 2024) and V-JEPA 2 (Ass-ran et al. 2025) extend it to action-conditioned planning. The original blueprint includes a latent variable for information left undetermined by a multimodal future (LeCun 2022). Re-leased systems omit that variable and remain deterministic. The released 305 M-parameter V-JEPA 2-AC checkpoint, for example, emits one next latent per state-action pair. We ver-ified this behavior with the released checkpoint and authors’ code. We study the effect of this choice and change only the predictor, not the encoder or its anti-collapse machinery (Bardes, Ponce, and LeCun 2022). 

Probabilistic JEPA. Huang et al. (2026) argue JEPA should be probabilistic and add a variational single-Gaussian predictive distribution. We benchmark a cosine-trained, residual-calibrated implementation, called Var-JEPA here to avoid confusion with Meta’s V-JEPA. MoE predictors in JEPA. M3-JEPA (Lei et al. 2024) im-plements the predictor as a gated MoE fused by a weighted sum into one output. The fusion remains subject to the single-output restriction in Prop. 2; increasing the expert count does not expose multiple successors to the planner. 

Multiple-choice and mixture regression. MCL/WTA (Guzman-Rivera, Batra, and Kohli 2012; Lee et al. 2016) and mixture density networks (Bishop 1994) address one-to-many regression. We apply this idea to JEPA world-model planning, diagnose collapse, and add controls absent from oracle-coverage evaluation. These controls test whether the recovered modes form context-dependent real transitions. 

Stochastic world models beyond JEPA. Reconstruction-based world models make the transition generative :Dreamer’s RSSM samples discrete stochastic latents (Hafner et al. 2021, 2023), and diffusion world models sam-ple observations (Alonso et al. 2024). These models can sample a multimodal law, but obtaining a finite set of rep-resentative successors may require multiple rollouts. MoP-JEPA retains decoder-free latent regression and returns its candidate set in one pass. 

Offline GCRL (external anchor). OGBench’s published baselines (Park et al. 2025, 2023; Wang et al. 2023; Eysen-bach et al. 2022; Ghosh et al. 2021) solve these tasks with reward-free policy learning. We do not claim to beat this line. Their published numbers provide external context for real-environment execution.

Verified-Route Evaluation 

We evaluate every method that emits multiple successors with the same protocol. planAll runs graph search over pre-dicted successors, while official-goal success restricts goals to the held-out OGBench set. For realroute (verified-route success), the model first proposes a transition graph without ground truth. Evaluation then asks whether that graph con-tains any start-to-goal route made entirely of real transitions. 

Transition precision is the fraction of predicted edges that are real, and execution success measures closed-loop plan-ning. Three controls test context dependence. A codebook ig-nores the input, shuffled contexts break the input-output pairing, and router gating keeps only heads with πk >

0.5/K .

Verified Planning Across the JEPA Predictor Family 

All arms in Table 4 differ only in the predictor module. The executor succeeds on 15 /15 true paths. With 10% false edges, replanning raises success from 0.40 to 1.00 . Transition precision identifies MDN’s non-existent edges, and the codebook exposes context-independent coverage in-flation. An off-manifold antmaze reference scored every method at zero. Real-sample manifolds correct the arti-fact. A +36% taxi-forecasting gain is 84% reproducible by the codebook and is rejected. We also report no gain on persistence-dominated video. 

Discussion and Limitations 

Implications for scaling JEPA world models. Video-and robot-scale world models (Assran et al. 2025; Zhou et al. 2024) are trained on data where stochastic transitions are common. A deterministic predictor may produce a compro-mise at branchings that matter for planning. MoP-JEPA in-stead supplies multiple candidate successors at the predictor output, independently of encoder scale. The same transition-validity checks can be applied to mixture, variational, diffu-sion, and other multi-future world models. 

Limitations. No model can select an aleatoric branch be-fore it occurs. We claim only to represent the transition dis-tribution. WTA is seed-bimodal on the hardest maze, con-sistent with the initialization sensitivity of Lloyd-style op-timization. Training loss separates the two basins in 20 /20 

runs because collapsed runs have zero router branch entropy. Restart selection obtains planAll 0.90 and realroute 0.35 in one selected run. Our tables report unselected means. The basin hit rate is 2–8 of 10 , and this restart rule is validated only on the current task family. At four-way branchings, successors below 10% proba-bility may remain inactive. Full four-mode recovery oc-curs in 37% of such cells. Annealing does not fix this at scale, head repulsion hurts, and rare-mode replay and head-specific learning rates remain untested. The main mazes are low-dimensional. Antmaze tests 29-D states with weaker gains, and DINO-WM tests pixel ob-servations. Conclusion 

A deterministic JEPA predictor represents a stochastic tran-sition with one regression optimum, whereas hard assign-ment permits different heads to represent different succes-sors. Across the evaluated domains, this distinction is re-flected in latent geometry and in the validity of predicted planning graphs, though optimization remains sensitive to initialization and rare modes. Evaluations of multimodal world models should therefore measure context dependence and transition validity in addition to raw coverage.
